Case Study:
UX Style Guide

Building a self-documenting style guide for Bluetooth

September 30, 2017

Overview

Role: UX Engineer - contractor - 8 weeks

Task: Design & develop a style guide that the entire company can leverage

I was contracted to improve the existing style guide tool, but there were many issues with the tool as described below. In the following sections I'll discuss a few of these issues and what steps I took to address them.

The problems, impacts, and goals were determined during a project planning phase. Many of the problems were complaints that the UX team had received but were unable to address with the existing implementation.

Although I was hired to make recommendations and to complete work on the existing tool, after conducting user interviews it quickly became apparent that the tool they had was too much work to maintain and was inadequate to meet their needs.

Through the interview process, the UX team was able to identify a number of issues surrounding the style guide and its usage, and the primary issues are documented in the next section.

Problem & Goal Statements

The problems we felt we could address through improvements to the style guide are highlighted below. The remainder of the issues will be addressed by the UX team through policy updates and content inventories.

Problems:

  • Developers aren't implementing styles from the guide
  • There are too many disparate styles and style sheets
  • There are disparate styles and UI components across platforms and tools
  • The style guide provides no definition on when/why to use components
  • The style guide website is not fully responsive
  • The existing style guide is poorly coded

Impacts:

  • User interfaces are inconsistently implemented and harder to maintain
  • Multiple style sheets decreases performance and creates confusion around which styles belong to which properties
  • Users of our products and tools are subject to an inconsistent, poor user experience
  • The style guide website is inconvenient for developers to use
  • The style guide website is difficult for designers to use and maintain

Goals:

  • Improved consistency of style implementation
  • Develop a unified set of styles
  • Clearly define usage guidelines around components
  • Remove obstacles to style guide usage
  • Improve underlying style guide code

Research: User Interviews

Our initial round of interviews conducted with influential developers helped us identify some workflow issues and a general lack of awareness of the intent of the style guide. We were also able to confirm the frustrations with the guide that had been expressed by its few users prior to my start.

Reviewing our research data, I was able to identify 3 unique user personas:

  1. Developers using Bootstrap
  2. Developers on other platforms
  3. Designers, Marketing, QA, and Management

Case Study:
Game Prototyping

Building a UX Proof-of-Concept for Bob Books

January 20, 2017

Overview

Thrilled with my work on their new font, Adam, the marketing director at Bob Books asked me to make a bid for their next game development contract.

Adam asked me to create a proposal that would demonstrate other gameplay methods and generally make the games more engaging.

Over the following month I designed a variety of spelling games; wrote a script; created illustrations, sound effects, and animations; and programmed a game in Unity with my friend Michael Afrides.

Research

Bob Books' existing games feature a single gameplay concept and individual spelling challenges. Each level is composed of simple line drawings and lacks any animation or color.

I believe that by telling a story with the challenges interspersed, the curiosity about story could become the compelling reason for progressing through the levels as opposed to simply completing each challenge for its own sake.

Click here to see Bob Books' original app that we were competing against.

Gameplay Concept 1

To win the contract, we would need to show a better UX while also proving that we could reproduce their current gameplay modes.

The basic gameplay mode required spelling a word in left-to-right order. This is an easy task, so we spiced it up a little with some 3D animations.

Watch Gameplay (16s)

Gameplay Concept 2

We also wanted to show something new and different that would still be something a young child could accomplish.

Our second gameplay mode drops "random" letters from the top of the screen. To add a little silliness and playfulness, the letters are trapped inside raindrops, which the player has to catch and drop onto sponges to wring out the water.

We also used this opportunity to create an animated 'cut-scene' - a feature the existing games don't have.

Watch Gameplay (26s)

Gameplay Concept 3

To create a sense of accomplishment, I wanted each section of our proposed game to feel like an immersive experience with hidden animations and environmental changes that reflect game progress.

We created a challenge navigation screen (this farm scene) where the rain diminishes and the cats and dogs stop 'raining' once you save them.

By the end of the demo the rain has cleared and the scene is pleasant and sunny.

Watch Home Screen Interaction (15s)

Conclusion

In the end the client decided to stick with their overseas developers who work for far less than we were willing to, so we didn't get the contract. Although it was disappointing that we weren't hired, it was a great experience creating this game from scratch on a new platform, and Michael used his experience here and at his next job to get a full-time game development role.

Case Study:
Collecting User Data

Learning about customers in ways that build trust

March 14, 2017

Overview

During my time at Amazon, one of my major projects revolved around learning how to collect demographic data from customers. The research I conducted is protected under NDA, so I can't disclose any specifics, but I can share the process and general learnings.

The Challenge

Some of the obvious solutions for collecting information are to ask for the information on user sign-up, to provide a gift in exchange for the information, or requiring the information in exchange for access to certain features.

Over the course of my research, I identified 5 key challenges to collecting demographic information from customers and designed solutions to address them.

The challenges I identified are detailed below.

  1. Cost of acquisition
  2. Information accuracy
  3. Coverage percentage
  4. Preserving customer trust
  5. Overcoming customer fear

Cost of Acquisition

Additionally, while many companies may see great results in acquiring information like birthdays or ages from customers in exchange for annual birthday gifts, there's actually very little those companies can do to assure the validity of the information. Case in point, at least one man has 365 registered Starbucks cards, each with a different birthday so he gets a free drink everyday. Not only can this be a very expensive proposition for the company, but it opens a "one-way door" to giving away gifts that can be very difficult to close again.

Information Accuracy

In a similar issue, Facebook (which has been very successful in collecting demographic data) has provided no incentive for truthful information. Even if Facebook were successful in collecting demographic data from 100% of their user base, I would argue that the data they've collected is almost worse than useless. Not only does it cost money to store all of that information, but without incentive to provide truthful answers, the data they have has such a high potential inaccuracy that any conclusions drawn would be suspect at best.

Coverage Percentage

Another challenge is collecting enough customer data to be able to draw meaningful conclusions. Even if you're only interested in the behaviors and habits of a particular demographic, unless you collect data from the majority of your user base, it can be difficult to determine what percentage of your customers in your target demographic you've collected information from.

Preserving Customer Trust

Customers we talked to were very comfortable with the idea of Amazon having their demographic information, but in spite of that, asking for customer information with little or no context made customers feel skeptical and wary. Essentially we found that if customers believed they had volunteered the information as in a profile, that was fine, but when we asked for it, it made them concerned about our intent.

Overcoming Customer Fear

Beyond concerns about our intent, customers in our studies consistently expressed concerns about being "profiled". Even believing that Amazon could provide them more interesting or relevant results, customers were afraid that we might do so by hiding results that we deemed irrelevant.

The Solution

To address cost of acquisition, we needed to provide a perceived benefit or value of some sort. This is relatively easy to do for free with digital services, but without a benefit directly related to the information collected, there's still no incentive for customers to provide truthful answers.

If we're interested in collecting customer birthdays, for example, there's very little we can do to tie a benefit to that. A solution needs to be relatively generic to appeal to a large percentage of the population, but each day of the year is so arbitrary that there's almost no specific value we can provide - hence the guy with a free birthday drink everyday.

Instead of tackling birthday head-on, we might first collect customer ages. Age is related to birthday because it advances on your birthday, but also can be far more telling about a customer's life-stage. To collect age, you might offer to show a customer things that other people their age are interested in. It's true that I could still tell you that I'm 80 years old and foul up that data, but as a customer, it benefits me to tell you my correct age. Especially in cases where providing an answer is not required, answering dishonestly will not benefit me in any way, and may actually hurt me by skewing my results (in this case, toward the interests of someone 50 years my senior).

Once a customer has provided their age, we can more subtly "nudge" them to provide their birthday so that we can accurately advance their age each year.

Customers feel safe providing their information when there is a clear value associated with it, so offering a tangible, directly-related benefit will always protect customer trust.

There is an additional challenge that comes up with the 'age' solution above, however, and that is what we call a "cold-start problem".

Cold-Start Problem

The concept here is simple; until we've collected a substantial amount of customer data, we can't provide meaningful information to the customers.

Every business that relies on customer input faces this challenge, and there are many ways of overcoming it. It's up to each organization to determine the appropriate solution and pacing for their cold-start problem, but the general approaches come down to focusing on a single group or grouping everyone together.

When it comes to grouping everyone, first you might show all people the same results, then later divide the customers into "Over 50" and "Under 50". When you have more data yet, divide ages by decade, and so on.

Conclusion

Taking all of these challenges into account and addressing each with your solution can strongly benefit your collected data and help you earn trust with your customers.

Each interaction your customers have with you contributes to your cumulative relationship. Handle with care.
