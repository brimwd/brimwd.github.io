Should YouTube Videos Be Eligible for Emmys? I have some thoughts...

About a week ago I encountered the YouTube CEO's guest-column for the Hollywood Reporter declaring It's time for the Emmys to Embrace Creators", and I've been thinking about it ever since. "Is it time?" Why is it time?

After a week of contemplation and research into the Emmys categories & awards; nomination & voting process; and the various organizations involved... I have some thoughts.

The Emmys undoubtedly could add another category for user-generated content (and maybe they will one day), but I’m not convinced that the current structure of the awards really fits in this case.

First, the Emmys primarily focus on content made and distributed in the USA, and that’s split into National or Regional awards depending on whether they reach more or less than half of Americans.

YouTube is available to almost everyone with internet access, so presumably creators would have to compete in the American “National” or the “International” awards.

The Sports, News & Documentary, and Children’s & Family categories could potentially nominate YouTube creators; I don’t know enough about how those categories are defined to say.

That said, there are several shows that started life on YouTube including Adventure Time, which was picked up by Cartoon Network and went on to win 8 Emmys...

In a way, you could say that “YouTube shows are already eligible once they reach a threshold of notoriety”. Kind of fits with the existing requirements around “reach” for National programming.

Presumably, to address the American focus, YouTube creators would need to be based in the USA to qualify for most awards. Given the substantially higher bar to be aired during “prime time”, non-sports/kids/news content would probably need to compete in the “daytime” category. I could be wrong, though; Apple TV’s (streaming-only) Ted Lasso won Primetime’s “Outstanding Comedy Series” in 2021 and 2022.

In any case, relatively few awards are given each year: one for each of Outstanding Comedy and Drama series, and the rest are acting, writing, production-design, etc.

One of the examples cited in the article, a YouTube series called “Challenge Accepted”, probably doesn’t qualify for any of the awards since it doesn’t seem to be heavily scripted, or “produced” in the traditional sense, and it’s mainly filmed “on location” while Michelle does ~something~.

No scripts means it wasn’t really “acted” so much as it was “recorded”. Maybe it would qualify for an “unstructured Reality TV” award? But in that case, Michelle’s up against the likes of RuPaul’s Drag Race.

“Challenge Accepted” was one of the few other examples cited in the article: specifically that the show has garnered “640 million views”... but that seems to be inaccurate.

“Challenge Accepted” has 22 episodes, and after adding up the view counts listed for each episode, I came to a total of ~144.3 million views (±2-3 million). That’s nothing to scoff at, but it’s not “640 million” either.

That number of “views” also doesn’t take into account that the total has been accumulated over the course of 6 years, nor can we differentiate between people who watched most of an episode vs those who clicked away after a few seconds.

Michelle is doing something great and she’s really got an audience... but I think there’s a case to be made that she’s already receiving a hefty reward from YouTube in terms of ad-revenue... not to mention the literal award(s) she’s received from YouTube for clearing 100k and 1 million subscribers.

Also cited, “Good Mythical Morning”, has 18.7 million subscribers (awesome!) but their videos typically garner less than 1 million views.

MKBHD, by contrast, has 18.9 million subscribers, but regularly clears 5 million views per video.

That could be a difference of content volume (GMM’s 21 episodes this month vs MKBHD’s 5), but again, who knows about actual watch-durations.

All of this is to say that creators on YouTube are doing great things, and they’ve already found ways of migrating to larger platforms (as with Cobra Kai, Broad City, Adam Ruins Everything, and Adventure Time) and receiving Emmys where the industry has seen merit.

YouTube creators are also doing great in their own right, with many of the most-successful creators becoming millionaires from ad-revenue and sponsorships.

It’s fantastic that YouTube has created a system where anyone can create and the market can dictate how the content is funded. That’s true content-democracy.

What I don’t see is a reason for the Emmy Awards to change their focus away from recognizing and rewarding broadcast television. The gauntlet of bureaucracy, producers, and red-tape is enough to destroy most content, and the recognition is as much a reward for the creators as it is a reward for the studios and stations that had the guts to produce and broadcast it.

It’s thanks to YouTube’s lack red-tape that our wild, creative, and often not-so-advertiser-friendly content can still get out into the world and thrive.

Rather than trying to force their way in, it would be great to see YouTube double-down on their own efforts to reward and recognize great content and creators on YouTube.

If YouTube really wants to be included in a broadcast awards ceremony, why not start their own? YouTube could partner with Vimeo, TikTok, Nebula, and other services.

Heck, they could even partner with Disney+, Netflix, Apple TV+, and the big studios too, and host a streaming-focused awards show.

To create market differentiation and give YouTube an edge, there could be rules stipulating conditions like “content has to be available online to stream”. They could really shake things up by allowing content from any year (fits better with the streaming model), but could require that the content is newly available to stream “this year” if it wasn’t already available.

The YouTube sponsored Streaming Awards could even include extra award categories for content that’s available to stream for free “to promote democratizing access” (the YouTube way).

YouTube also has a great opportunity to start a “Path to Broadcast” program for Creators to get discovered by broadcasters. YouTube could form official partnerships with “premium” services to share licensing, collaborate on marketing, or just to build connections that will help the Creators on their journey.

YouTube could even stay involved in the negotiations to ensure that the content still streams on YouTube and that the creators don’t lose the rights to their stories or characters.

YouTube could become not-only the best place to publish content, but the best champions for our content.
That’s the YouTube I want to watch.

Honestly, who even needs the Emmys?
