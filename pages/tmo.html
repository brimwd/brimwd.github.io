T-Mobile Cloud ‘Conducktor’

Platform-as-a-Service for everyone

February 9, 2019

Overview

Even for expert developers, deploying applications to the cloud is messy and complicated; my challenge was to make it feel intuitive and easy.

Starting From Scratch

I joined T-Mobile in January of 2018, shortly after the architect completed development of a 'Proof of Concept' for their proposed Platform-as-a-Service (PaaS) platform.

What is PaaS?

PaaS allows you to install and run software without having to manage any of the details like networking, runtime, operating-system, servers, storage, networking, etc. A nice analogy is the idea of running an app on your phone; PaaS is like your phone operating system which takes care of power-management, multitasking, etc so the developers can just focus on building their apps.

My job was to collaborate with the Architect and the Product Manager to define the roadmap and project scope for multiple releases, draft the various data models and relationships necessary to support the platform, define the personas, define the workflows, manage the interface development, and finally to design and support development of the UI.

Building Relationships

(click to expand) collapse

Before I could build an interface for our product, I needed to determine what tasks our users needed to complete (fundamentally what our system was supposed to do), and after that, which options or configurations we'd need to support.

Because our product was intended for Enterprise-deployment, we needed to support a generic, extensible data model so other companies building on our platform could define their own 'staging' requirements. We also needed to figure out what data our own team needed in order to approve and promote customer applications from Proof-of-Concept (PoC) to Production.

Finally, because this is a 'managed' system with several different types of users, I interviewed our architect, stakeholders, and the customer-administration team to define our personas and determine what access each persona would need.

Proof of Concept

From engaging with users and assessing the challenge I was given when I was hired ("make deploying to the cloud feel easy"), I knew we would need a graphical UI that supported tutorials, tool-tips, helper text, input-constraints, and other user-friendly features.

When it came to actually delivering a web-interface, I was frustrated to discover that nobody on the team wanted a UI. Our architect often said "I know our users and they want a CLI" (CLI stands for Command-Line-Interface - basically "MS Dos" or the "Terminal" application on your computer), and our engineers agreed.

Example: Using a CLI to purchase a web-domain (~15s)

In my experience, CLIs can be exceptionally difficult to use because you need to remember all of the commands (or individually look up each one). Additionally, rather than simply clicking on a few familiar icons to complete a task, a CLI requires you to type in every command -- and to enter them in the correct sequence. Finally, a CLI is limited to showing a single piece of data at a time; forget alerts, real-time updates, and multi-tasking -- you need to manually type in requests for each inquiry. Talk about "unfriendly".

I knew I couldn't win them over up-front, but realized that if the CLI and Platform teams developed features independently, redundant work might occur or worse, the two could fall out of sync. As a remedy, I suggested that the CLI should merely "wrap the API" (meaning that all of the functions would be built directly into the Platform, and the CLI would simply be the interface for accessing that functionality).

The engineers agreed to this approach, and "to facilitate our discussions" I began presenting low-fidelity UI wireframes in conversations about features, sequencing, and permissions.

Manage Anywhere

(click to expand) collapse

During the first weeks after the UI launch, I noticed many of the Platform Devs squeezing the UI window down to its smallest size and toggling back-and-forth between the UI and their other work.

Although I received push-back from the architect about supporting a 'responsive' design, I received my manager's blessing to partner with the UI Developer to optimize our UI to work on mobile devices (in case our customers needed to check their applications on the go) and especially for the 500px breakpoint (the narrowest size on Google's Chrome browser - our users' preferred browser).

Project Impact

By focusing on user needs, including those of the engineers developing the Platform, our UI received wide-adoption throughout the company. We also enjoyed some remarkable metrics, including a reduction in 'New User On-boarding' time from 3 months to under 10 minutes (a ~99% reduction).

Through the development of more-accurate, concise, and usable documentation and the development of an intake-"wizard", support requests were reduced from a series of eight or more contacts requiring 2-4 hours per step down to a single 1-hour review session with each customer prior to publishing a new application.

Having a responsive interface that could be used side-by-side while working also accelerated development and improved incident-response times for the core-platform team, and improved architecture-and-design documentation demystified the API and extensibility-configurations for developers of 'add-ons'.

Support for 14,000 APIs, 50 applications, fielding 1 billion API requests per day, and saving $50+ million per year on hosting and infrastructure.
