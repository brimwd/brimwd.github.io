Case Study:
Amazon ‘Instant Fulfillment’

Solving for find-ability and discoverability in Amazon’s “Instant Fulfillment” Browse Taxonomy

08, 14, 2019

Most sites have miserable information architectures that mirror the way the company internally thinks about the content and not the way users think about the content. Predictably, users ignore such unhelpful structure.

Jakob Nielsen, January 8, 2000“Is Navigation Useful”

Shopping in an Instant

Opportunity

Customers visiting a ‘Campus Pickup Point’ are idle for a short time awaiting their package.

Students visiting may want to buy a snack or pick up school supplies on their way to class.

Goal

Empower customers to instantly buy any of over 2,000 curated items.

IA should facilitate organic discovery, learn-ability of categories, and location-specific product offerings.

Problem Statement

Amazon is not ‘shop’-able:

From redundant listings to miscategorized products and a taxonomy reflecting business-units instead of the ways people shop and buy, customers can’t browse for products.

A Fork in the Road

Knowing customers couldn’t effectively shop on Amazon, we had two choices: Use it anyway or define our own.

  • Utilizing the global taxonomy
  • Customers only need to learn one system
  • No additional taxonomy work is necessary
  • Categories might contain only one product
  • Empty categories need to be filtered from results
  • Products are frequently miscategorized
  • Defining our own structure
  • Few customers know the global structure
  • Opportunity to simplify the taxonomy
  • Not saddled with nearly-empty categories
  • No need to correct miscategorized products
  • Alternative taxonomy must be designed

Testing Round 1:

Performing usability testing on the UI, I observed that although participants were able to complete our ‘happy path’ tasks, many of the participants were noticeably hesitant to browse for products.

We started the usability tests with a disclaimer “we’re testing a new idea: if it’s difficult or confusing, that’s a sign that we still need to improve our design”, but participants were still noticeably hesitant as they browsed.

Observing them, where we hoped they would be excited to explore, but instead it felt like they were afraid of choosing the wrong categories.

My intuition told me it would be difficult for customers to learn the taxonomy, and the way the UI was designed, there was too little information scent to encourage discovery.

Inflection Point

Before I could as for a change, I’d need to make a compelling argument case, and that meant tearing down the current solution and offering up something better.

Understand

“What’s the current solution? How did we get here?”

  • Arbitrary categories
  • Data-driven product selection
  • Improving costs more than adapting

Find Opportunities

“What can we do with what we have?”

  • Improve categories
  • Choose products intentionally
  • Push changes back to Amazon core

(Im)prove it

“Exceeding 95% confidence.”

  • Propose an alternative solution
  • Demonstrate results in a reliable and repeatable way

Eureka!

And then it hit me — there’s no hard requirement that products exist in only one category.

If I can’t find a ‘one size fits all’ solution, “No problem”.

We can just put every ‘questionable’ item in every matching category.

Clearly a polyhierarchy is the way to go.

Testing Round 2:

Participant info:

  • Source: Amazon interns
  • Gender: 10 male, 10 female
  • Ages: 18–24

Task:

Participants used a simple nested-list (in which only one level of the list was visible at a time) to browse for products. Each participant was required to browse for 10 different products (from one of two lists).

Success Criteria:

Participants were given up to two chances to guess the matching category and sub-category for each product in the list.

So How’d I do?

“Whoops!”

Research, research, research

Utilizing and refining my draft guidelines as I worked, I conducted 8 additional rounds of tests.

Amazon’s bar of confidence is 95%. It’s a lofty goal, but I was confident that I could make it if I kept working.

Open Card-Sort

Using the names of 50 products I selected to represent the 2000 on offer, participants sorted cards into categories ranging in size from 3 to 15 items.

>20 totally-unexpected categories emerged

Tree Testing

Using the dozens of categories generated in the card-sort, I selected the 35 most-distinct labels that covered the broadest swath of items.

12 participants browsed for 20 of 35 products

Re-testing

5 additional rounds of testing with 6–10 participants moved us incrementally closer to 95% confidence.

Final Round:

10 participants, 20 items

“What is this?”

The ‘elusive’ item that all 3 failures had in common
